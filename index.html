Visualization Rationale

We chose a choropleth map as the primary encoding because spatial distribution is the core question: where does California green up first? Color encodes NDVI/EVI magnitude using a diverging brown-to-green scale, mirroring the natural semantic of the data (brown = dry, green = vegetated). Position (geography) encodes spatial variation, which is the most perceptually accurate channel for location data.

The time slider enables temporal brushing — a dynamic query interaction that lets users directly observe seasonal change without cognitive overhead. The play/pause animation further supports pattern recognition by letting vegetation cycles unfold naturally. We considered small multiples (12 static maps) but found they required too much visual comparison work; the animation is far more effective at conveying temporal dynamics.

The click-to-county time series chart adds a details-on-demand layer. Rather than overwhelming the main view with line charts, we reserve them for user-initiated exploration. Showing both NDVI and EVI simultaneously allows comparison of the two indices — EVI corrects for soil background and atmospheric effects, providing a more conservative measure in sparse vegetation areas like the Central Valley.

Color palette: dark background reduces eye strain for extended exploration and makes the green gradient more vivid. The gold accent (slider, selected county) creates a clear figure-ground hierarchy without competing with the NDVI color scale.

Data & Transformations

Data source: NASA MODIS MOD13A3 product, accessed via NASA Earthdata. Monthly composite vegetation indices (NDVI, EVI) at 1km resolution for California (32°N–42°N, 124°W–114°W) across all 12 months of 2025. Five MODIS tiles were combined and reprojected. Per-county aggregates (mean NDVI/EVI) were computed after masking fill values and water pixels (~1.2–1.5M valid pixels/month). County boundaries are approximated for visualization.
